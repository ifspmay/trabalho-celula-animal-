What are cells?

A cell is the smallest structural and functional unit of all living beings, a microscopic living system that can exist as a complete organism (unicellular) or be part of a larger organism (multicellular).

An animal cell

is the basic unit of animals and humans, being a type of eukaryotic cell that has a well-defined nucleus and various organelles, such as mitochondria, lysosomes, and endoplasmic reticulum, within its cytoplasm.

See the organelles

The nucleus

is the "control center" of eukaryotic cells (animals, plants, fungi, etc.), storing DNA (genetic material) and controlling cell activities, such as protein production and cell division.

The plasma membrane

is a thin film that surrounds the cell, composed mainly of a lipid bilayer (phospholipids) and proteins, as well as carbohydrates in the glycocalyx. Its primary function is selective permeability, controlling the transport of substances into and out of the cell, maintaining internal stability.

The cytoplasm

is the region of the cell between the plasma membrane and the nucleus. It is mainly composed of the cytosol (a gel-like fluid), cellular organelles, and other structures such as the cytoskeleton.

The mitochondria

are organelles of eukaryotic cells that function as "power plants," generating most of the chemical energy (ATP) needed for cellular activities through cellular respiration. They have two membranes (outer and inner) and their own DNA and ribosomes.

The endoplasmic reticulum

is a network of interconnected membranes in eukaryotic cells, essential for the transport of proteins and lipids, and for cellular metabolism. It is divided into:

The rough endoplasmic reticulum

is an organelle formed by a network of membranes with ribosomes attached to its surface. Its main function is to produce and modify proteins that can be sent out of the cell, incorporated into the plasma membrane, or used in organelles such as lysosomes.

The smooth endoplasmic reticulum

is formed by a network of tubules without ribosomes. It has important functions such as the production of lipids and steroid hormones, detoxification of harmful substances, and carbohydrate metabolism.

The Golgi complex

is formed by stacks of flattened membranous sacs. Its main function is to organize, modify, and package proteins and lipids produced by the endoplasmic reticulum, preparing them for use within the cell or for export outside it.

The lysosomes

are cellular organelles in the form of vesicles that contain digestive enzymes, acting as the "digestive system" of the cell. Their main function is the digestion of materials foreign to the cell (heterophagy) and the recycling of obsolete or damaged cellular components (autophagy).

The ribosomes

are small organelles present in all types of cells. They are responsible for protein synthesis, reading the instructions of messenger RNA and assembling amino acids in the correct order. They can be free in the cytoplasm or attached to the rough endoplasmic reticulum, depending on where the protein will be used.

The centrioles

are cylindrical organelles found in animal cells. They play a fundamental role in cell division, helping to organize the mitotic spindle, which separates chromosomes during mitosis and meiosis. Additionally, they participate in the formation of cilia and flagella, structures important for cell movement.

The peroxisomes

are small organelles enclosed by a single membrane, found in eukaryotic cells, that contain oxidative enzymes, such as catalase, to detoxify the cell, metabolize fats, and synthesize phospholipids.

The vacuoles

are small vesicles enclosed by a membrane that have functions such as storing substances (like waste and nutrients), aiding in cellular digestion, and helping with osmotic regulation. Unlike the large central vacuole of plant cells, animal vacuoles are generally smaller, more numerous, and temporary.
